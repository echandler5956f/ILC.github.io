BiQu is a long-standing WPI project on quadrupedal locomotion.

Our goal is to further the field of legged loco-manipulation. To accomplish this, we need hardware which is dynamic enough to execute our controllers, and robust enough to not break when our controllers encounter unmodeled disturbances. We are currently using a Unitree Go1 quadruped, which was generously provided by Unitree and the WPI RBE department.

BiQu original hardware.

BiQu (Bimodal Quadruped) was originally a Major Qualifying Project at WPI. The goal was to explore multi-modal locomotion, including getting a quadruped to stand on two legs. The original five-person BiQu team built a 12-DoF quadruped based on the Solo-12 design, and created BiQu Software, which allowed the robot to perform backflips and walking gaits in simulation. Inspired by this work, several M.S. students at WPI continued the project over the summer, and created their own backflip controller using a pseudospectral collocation method. This work was presented at WPI's EREE.

BiQu Acrobatics.

During EREE, the BiQu team used a pseudospectral collocation method to solve the optimal control problem associated with a backflip. The input to the optimizer is the intial and final state, as well as a rotation matrix reference trajectory. The optimal control solver identifies the necessary contact forces, foot positions, and contact timings to successfully perform the backflip, while satisfying the dynamic constraints which govern the Single Rigid Body approximation of the quadruped robot.

HURON
WPI's custom-built humanoid

Simultaneous to the work on BiQu in 2022-23, a WPI team of 13 B.S. students designed and built a custom bipedal robot, with plans to add an upper body. Over the following summer, a team of 4 M.S. students designed controllers for push recovery and taking steps in simulation using sliding mode control and pseudospectral collocation, respectively.

In this video you can see the HURON hardware walking in the real world. It should be noted however this video was made with predefined, open-loop joint trajectories. Our more recent work, while not yet deployed on the real robot, reasons about the whole body dynamics and kinematics.

Galileo:
A product of BiQu and HURON collaboration

As of 2023-2024, BiQu and HURON have worked hand-in-hand to develop Galileo: WPI's in-house psueodspectral collocation solver for hybrid systems. An example of the types of problems Galileo may solve include legged robot locomotion, optimally driving an autonomous vehicle, or even flying a drone while avoiding obstacles.

Galileo is:
  • versatile in that it is designed to solve any continuous variable optimal control problem for rigid body systems written in Bolza form, making it applicable to quadrupeds, humanoid robots, drones, autonomous vehicles, and more.
  • efficient, using powerful yet lightweight libraries such as pinocchio and CasADi to accurately compute the complex floating base dynamics, and the symbolic gradients/Hessians of the optimal control problem. Preliminary results show promise of real-time nonlinear MPC capabilities.
  • lightweight and easy to install, containing mostly headers and relying only on the core ingredents from pinocchio and CasADi.
  • extensible, including several powerful interfaces to allow users to easily set up complex optimal control problems (including quadruped and biped locomotion) in as few as ~100 lines of code.
  • free-software, open-source and available on GitHub.

Perception in Galileo.

Galileo can utilize perception by incorporating information about the robot's surroundings in the problem's constraints. Using plane segmentation, we can classify steppable regions from point cloud data (recieved from a depth camera), and convert them into convex steppable regions which can be applied as linear constraints in Galileo. This will enable Galileo to reason about the environment in real time, avoiding obstacles and only stepping in regions which are flat enough to be safe.

Publications and Future Work

ILC's work in control for legged robots has led to a paper submission to ICRA 2024. We plan to submit two more conference papers in IROS + ICRA 2025 on Galileo. Our research goals currently encompass the combined tasks of locomotion and manipulation, dubbed: "loco-manipulation". Loco-manipulation for legged robots is one of the hardest problems in robotics. We intend to use our in-house switched system solver (Galileo) to build a multi-modal tree in real time which will allow us to solve both the optimal trajectory (a continuous problem) and the optimal contact sequence (a discrete problem) simultaneously. This approach was inspired by the minds at ETH-Zurich in their Versatile Multi-Contact Planning and Control for Legged Loco-Manipulation publication.
